# Aviation Terminology

## Callsign

A callsign is the spoken or operational identifier used by an aircraft on the radio and in many flight tracking systems. A callsign may match a flight number, but not always. For example, a United flight might use a callsign like `UAL123`.

## Squawk Code

A squawk code is a four-digit transponder code assigned to an aircraft by air traffic control. The code helps radar systems identify aircraft and track them more easily.

Important details:

- Squawk codes are four octal digits, meaning each digit ranges from 0 to 7.
- Air traffic control can assign a discrete squawk code to a specific aircraft.
- Some codes have special meanings.

Common special squawk codes:

- `7500` means hijacking or unlawful interference
- `7600` means radio communication failure
- `7700` means general emergency

When someone asks what a squawk code is, the core idea is that it is a transponder code used for aircraft identification and emergency signaling.

## IFR

IFR stands for Instrument Flight Rules. Under IFR, pilots fly primarily by reference to instruments and operate according to clearances and procedures managed by air traffic control. IFR is commonly used in poor weather, controlled airspace, and airline operations.

## VFR

VFR stands for Visual Flight Rules. Under VFR, the pilot generally flies by outside visual references and must maintain weather minimums that allow visual separation from terrain and other aircraft.

## Altitude

Altitude usually refers to an aircraft's height above mean sea level, expressed in feet in most US aviation contexts. Flight levels, such as FL350, refer to standardized pressure altitudes and are commonly used at higher altitudes.

## Transponder

A transponder is onboard equipment that replies to radar interrogation with identifying and altitude information. Squawk codes are set on the aircraft transponder.

## Taxi, Takeoff, and Climb

- Taxi: moving on the ground under the aircraft's own power
- Takeoff: acceleration and rotation from runway to flight
- Climb: ascending after departure to cruise altitude
